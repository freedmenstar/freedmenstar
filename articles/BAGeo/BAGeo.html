Population History & Genealogy

The Blood Geography of America

A review of federal census records, immigration data, and genealogical documentation examining the continuous population presence of distinct ancestry groups in the United States from 1607 to present.

Ronnie Bailey
March 2026
Data Analysis
```

This article draws on publicly available federal records, peer-reviewed demographic research, and genealogical archives to examine a specific question: which non-Indigenous population groups have maintained the longest continuous documented presence in the United States, and how do their growth histories compare across time.

The data sources used here — U.S. Census Bureau decennial records, Freedmen's Bureau registers, USCIS immigration statistics, and the slave schedules of 1860 — are primary government documents. The population figures they contain are not estimates or projections. They are enumerated counts, available for independent review at archives.gov and census.gov.

The Timeline

The timeline begins in 1607, when English settlers established the first permanent colony at Jamestown, Virginia. In 1619, the first documented Africans arrived in English North America at Point Comfort, Virginia. In the decades that followed, the African-origin population grew through continued forced migration. In 1808, the Act Prohibiting Importation of Slaves (Statutes at Large, 2:426) took effect, legally closing the international slave trade. From that date forward, the Black American population grew without outside influx — through natural increase alone — for the 57 years remaining before Emancipation, and in subsequent generations.

Separately, European immigration into the United States accelerated significantly beginning in the 1840s. USCIS historical records document the arrival of several million German and Irish immigrants between 1840 and 1880, followed by the Ellis Island period of 1880 to 1924, during which millions of Southern and Eastern European immigrants entered the country. These arrivals expanded the population enumerated in census records as white. The majority were not genealogically descended from the original English colonial settlers of the 1600s.

The charts below present this population history in two formats — raw counts for the founding-era period, and indexed growth trajectories from each group's point of arrival. All figures are cited to their source documents at the end of this article.
```

```
On Population Size vs. Length of Presence

The 2020 U.S. Census records approximately 204 million people identifying as white and approximately 41.8 million identifying as Black or African American. A common inference drawn from this disparity is that white Americans have historically represented the larger and more established population. The demographic record, however, distinguishes between the size of a population at a given point in time and the duration of its continuous presence. These are separate measurements.

Three Findings From the Record
01 The current white American population is not primarily descended from the original English colonial settlers. U.S. Census nativity tables and USCIS immigration records document that between 1840 and 1924, more than 30 million European immigrants entered the United States — the majority from Germany, Ireland, Italy, Poland, Russia, and the Austro-Hungarian Empire. Demographic historian Campbell Gibson's analysis of census data (Working Paper No. 56, 2002) confirms that this immigration substantially expanded the white American headcount. The pre-1700 English colonial line represents a proportionally small fraction of today's white American population.
02 The Black American population's growth rate was affected by documented historical constraints. The institution of slavery restricted family formation and imposed high mortality rates on the enslaved population. Following Emancipation, the destruction of Black-owned property documented in events such as the 1921 Tulsa Race Massacre, exclusion from the Servicemen's Readjustment Act of 1944, and documented housing discrimination under federal redlining policies each had measurable demographic effects on Black American population growth post-1865.
03 Genealogical continuity and population size are distinct measurements. The Black American population has grown without documented outside influx since 1808 — more than 200 years of internal generational growth. The white American population, during this same period, expanded significantly through immigration. Current census figures reflect cumulative arrivals across centuries. The question of which group has maintained the longest uninterrupted presence is answered by arrival and closure dates in the historical record, not by present-day headcounts.
```
