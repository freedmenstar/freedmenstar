What Jackson Ward Holds

A walk through the Black History Museum and Cultural Center of Virginia

Ronnie Bailey
October 2024
People & Places Richmond, Virginia

Jackson Ward sits in Richmond the way old things tend to sit — quietly, without announcing itself, letting whoever passes decide how much attention to pay. The sign at the entrance calls it a National Historic Landmark District. That's accurate and also undersells it considerably.

The Black History Museum and Cultural Center of Virginia is inside that district, on Clay Street, in a building that manages to feel both permanent and purposeful at the same time. I went on a weekday. The parking lot was mostly empty. The entrance is unassuming — brick, glass, a banner with the BHMVA logo. Inside is something else.

The Building

The museum occupies a converted armory. You feel the bones of it when you walk in — high ceilings, exposed beams, wide open gallery floors. What was built to store weapons now stores something more durable. The architects kept the industrial shell and let the history fill it. That combination works.

What strikes you first is how much ground the collection covers. This is not a single-era institution. The museum moves through time the way a family moves through a house — room by room, generation by generation, with evidence of all of it still visible.

The Rooms

The Reconstruction era gallery is where the building feels most alive. The space is wide, lit from above by track lighting, and along the walls are digital displays and framed documents and a historical marker for John Mitchell Jr., the Richmond Planet editor who used his newspaper to document and resist during a period when that combination required extraordinary nerve. His marker stands in the gallery like a piece of the street brought inside.

The Jim Crow era wall is dense. Photographs arranged in clusters, archival and unsparing. A store front, a portrait, a group of men seated together. Text panels with W.E.B. Du Bois and Booker T. Washington. The weight of this section comes not from shock but from accumulation — the sheer number of lives documented, the ordinary detail of each one.

"Once let the black man get upon his person the brass letters, U.S., let him get an eagle on his button, and a musket on his shoulder and bullets in his pocket, there is no power on earth, or under the earth that can deny that he has earned the right to citizenship in the United States."

Frederick Douglass, July 6, 1863

One photograph stops you cold. Five generations of one enslaved family, photographed on the Smith Plantation in Beaufort, South Carolina, 1862. Five generations alive at the same time. The oldest and the youngest in the same frame. The museum's label is matter-of-fact about what it is. The photograph doesn't need any help.

Nearby, Powhatan Beaty — Richmond native, Sergeant, 5th U.S. Colored Infantry, Medal of Honor recipient, Battle of New Market Heights, September 29, 1864. His portrait is clean and formal. He looks directly at whoever is looking at him.

Arthur Ashe

There is a room with a sculpture of Arthur Ashe that is unlike any memorial I have seen for him. It is full-size, all white, rendered in a tracksuit, one arm raised holding a book, the other holding a tennis racket. The posture is triumphant without being theatrical. Ashe was born in Richmond. He grew up in segregated parks and tennis courts. The sculpture stands in a museum in the neighborhood that shaped the city that shaped him. The circularity of that is not subtle and doesn't need to be.

What Gets Kept

A bound journal from 1850 to 1888 sits in a glass case near a window. The handwriting is careful, dense, slanted. It belongs to John McGill, James Gibbons, and John Joseph Keane of the Catholic Diocese of Richmond. Outside the window is Jackson Ward. The journal recorded the same neighborhood it overlooks. That kind of continuity — object and place sharing the same physical space across 170 years — is what museums at their best actually do.

The community room holds large-format black and white photographs of Black social life — formal gatherings, dancing, crowds dressed for occasions. These photographs are not in cases. They hang on the wall the way family photos hang in a hallway. The effect is intentional and correct. This is not documentation of an extinct people. This is family.

The Woolworth's Counter

The museum has a recreation of a Woolworth's luncheonette — the booths, the chrome trim, the original signage mounted behind glass. The sit-ins happened at counters like this one. The museum doesn't dramatize it. The sign says Visit Woolworth's Luncheonette in the original font, in the original colors, and the ordinary cheerfulness of that advertisement is the whole point. The counter was ordinary. What happened at it was not. The recreation holds both things at once.

What It Is

The BHMVA is not a place that asks you to feel bad. It asks you to pay attention. The difference matters. The collection does not lead with suffering. It leads with presence — this person lived here, did this, built that, said this, survived this, kept going. The suffering is part of the record but it is not the point of it.

Jackson Ward produced Maggie Walker, the first woman to charter and serve as president of a bank in the United States. It produced Bill "Bojangles" Robinson. It produced a density of Black-owned enterprise that earned it the name the Black Wall Street of the South before that phrase was applied anywhere else. The museum holds that history without apology and without performance. It simply keeps it. That is enough.

I left through the same parking lot I came in through. The Jackson Ward sign was visible from the car. I had walked past it on the way in without stopping. I stopped on the way out.
